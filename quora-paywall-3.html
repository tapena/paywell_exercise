Do language learners develop a new identity as they learn to use a second language?

Yes, in some way.

I'm taking a class called The Politics of Language, and in that class we get to learn about how language influences our perception of ourselves. I agree that I do receive a new identity when I "switch the code" between languages.

David Crystal once wrote an essay called Vanishing Languages about endangered languages spoken by minority or submerged by stronger languages (say, Welsh and Irish vs English), in which he quotes a Welsh proverb: "A nation without a language is a nation without a heart." To him, when a language disappear, it's as if we're losing out a way of describing human condition. Such strong tie between language and cultural heritage shows that the same tie exists between the the language and a part of its speakers' identity, whether the speakers are native residents or foreigners.

I experience this as well when I come to America. When I speak different languages, I feel as if I'm entering different linguistic worlds in each I am engaged to a different manner. When I use English, I feel strong and expressive in writing or in speaking, as I'm learning how to write more concisely and forcibly everyday (yes, White&Trunk). When I speak Vietnamese, I feel the tender and the very dear sense of belonging, as if I'm living in the world of Vietnamese Literature, what I've been attached to for 18 years. When I speak French, I feel the beauty, both verbally and visually.

Also, there is an article about how language is linked to personality/identity. It's not to stereotype a language and its speakers, but each language has a characteristic that makes it special and worth pursuing.

Johnson: Do different languages confer different personalities?
